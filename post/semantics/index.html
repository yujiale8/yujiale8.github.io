A brief intro to Semantics

Semantics

The study of the meaning of words, phrases and sentences.

Conceptual and Associative Meaning

Conceptual Meaning covers these basic, essential components of meaning which are conveyed by the literal use of a word. E.g. needle is may include thin, sharp, steel instrument;
Associative Meaning is the idea, connection what that specific word brings to you. E.g. needle is related to painful

Componential Analysis

Componential analysis is a way proposed by the structural semanticists to analyze word meaning.

The approach is based upon the belief that the meaning of a word can be broken down into meaning components, called semantic features.

e.g. The hamburger ate the boy.
This sentence is syntactically perfect : S => NP + VP ( V + NP )
But the meaning is not acceptable. The verb and the subject do not relate each other.
We identify the meaning by analyzing some semantic features .

Semantic Roles

Agent: an entiy that performs an action (John ran.)

Theme: an entity that undergoes an action or is involved in an action or affected by an action or is simply described (Mary called Bill; The ball is red.)

Location: the place where an action takes place. (It rains in Spain)

Goal: the place where an action is directed. (Put the cat on the porch)

Source: the place from which an action originates. (He flew from Iowa to Idaho)

Instrument: the means by which an action is performed. (He cuts hair with a razor.)

Experiencer: an entity that perceives sth. (Helen heard Robert playing the piano.)

Lexical Relations

Synonymy

Words which share a likeness in denotation as well as in part of speech.
E.g. broad – wide, large – big

Antonymy

Antonymy is concerned with semantic opposition.
E.g. alive – dead, big – small

  • Gradable Antonyms: Antonyms that can be used in comparative constructions.
    E.g. bigger than – smaller than the negative of one member of the pair does not necessarily imply the other.
    e.g. That dog is not old. (It does not have to mean “ that dog is young“).
  • Non – Gradable Antonyms ( Complementary Pairs ): Comparative constructions are not normally used, and the negative of one member does imply the other.
    e.g. deader / more dead => not possible
    e.g. that person is not dead: that person is alive.
  • Reversives (Converse antonyms)
    They do the opposite of the other action.
    e.g. tie – untie, enter – exit

Hyponymy

When the meaning of one form is included in the meaning of another, the relationship is described as hyponymy.
e. g . rose – flower, carrot – vegetable
rose is a hyponymy of flower - carrot is a hyponymy of vegetable
Co – Hyponymy/ Super ordinate
Animal ( super ordinate ) => horse / dog / bird
Horse, dog, bird => co- hyponymies of animal

Prototypes

A prototype is the best example of a category.

Homophony

When two or more different written forms have the same pronunciation they are described as homophones.
E.g. bear – bare, meet – meat, write - right

Homonyms

We use the term homonymy when one form (written or spoken) has two or more unrelated meanings.
E.g. bank ( bank – of a river ), (bank – financial institution )

Polysemy

when one form ( written and spoken ) has multiple meanings which are all related by extension .
E.g. head => top of your body / top of a glass of beer / top of a company

Metonymy

A type of relation between words based simply on a close connection in everyday experience.
E.g. bottle – coke ( a container – contents relation )
car – wheels ( a whole – part relation )
king – crown ( a representative – symbol relation )

Collocation

the words that naturally go together.
E.g. hammer – nail
table – chair
salt – pepper
They frequently occur together.
